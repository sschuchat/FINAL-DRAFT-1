Marketing Strategy

We Don't Pull People In. We Meet Them Where They Already Are.

CivicCircle's growth strategy is inbound by design. Following the frameworks developed by HubSpot and detailed in David Meerman Scott's The New Rules of Marketing and PR, we create content and experiences valuable enough that our customers find us — not the other way around.

01
Attract

Useful, civic-minded content that shows up when people search for what's happening locally.

02
Engage

Conversations that treat residents and city officials as neighbors, not leads.

03
Delight

An experience worth telling a friend — our most important acquisition channel.

Section 1

Who We're Actually Writing For

Inbound marketing fails when it is pitched to a demographic average rather than to a real person. The three personas below are composites built from interviews, civic data, and the lived experiences of our own team and community. Every piece of content we produce is written for exactly one of them.

Lifestyle

Marcus moved to New Orleans six months ago for his first post-college engineering job. He works remotely three days a week, lives alone in a walk-up off Magazine Street, and is halfway between "I'm so glad I moved here" and "I still don't know anybody." He runs on Saturday mornings, cooks for himself, and treats live music as a primary form of social connection — the only reliable way he knows to be around other people without the pressure of a scheduled hangout.

Pain Points

  • The Friday night paralysis: spends 45 minutes jumping between Instagram, Facebook events, and local blogs trying to figure out what's happening tonight. Usually gives up and orders delivery.
  • Post-move loneliness: the apps that helped him find an apartment don't help him find a life. Civic engagement would help him build roots; he has no idea where to start.
  • Fear of missing out on "the real city": worries he's living a tourist version of the city. Wants local, not Eventbrite-touristy.
  • No word-of-mouth network yet: doesn't have the Friday-night group chat that longer-term residents rely on for recommendations.

Behavior & Interests

Heavy Instagram user; checks TikTok daily; rarely reads email newsletters but subscribes to two local ones. Listens to a dozen podcasts on long runs. Uses Spotify, Notion, and Apple Maps more than any other apps. Will install an app if a friend texts him a link; will not install an app from a social ad. Trusts human recommendations over algorithms but trusts algorithms more than he'd like to admit. Goes deep on niche interests — vinyl, indie brewing, small-venue music — and ignores mainstream content.

Why Marcus Buys In

Marcus doesn't need a better events app — he needs a shortcut to belonging. CivicCircle wins Marcus not by advertising features but by showing up in his life with content that reflects what he already values: a podcast episode about the history of a local venue, an Instagram reel from the sound engineer at a bar he's walked past twenty times, a newsletter item about a Saturday neighborhood cleanup that introduces him to people his own age. He installs the app not because we asked him to, but because the content we're already giving him for free is so useful he wants more of it in one place.

Lifestyle

Elena has been in municipal government for twenty-two years. She moved from press secretary work to communications director four years ago and carries real professional pride in the job — she genuinely cares about connecting her city to the people who live in it. Her day is half strategy and half triage: a mayor's tweet that needs cleanup, a public meeting that's being poorly attended, a local reporter asking about a budget item. She reads Route Fifty and GovTech over coffee, attends at least two civic tech conferences a year, and has been burned enough times by bad vendor pitches that she treats every cold email with mild skepticism.

Pain Points

  • The civic engagement crisis: fewer than 8% of residents attend a public meeting in a given year. Her engagement KPIs are flat and she has no new levers to pull.
  • Legacy tech stack: the city website is seven years old, was built by a vendor that was acquired twice, and the analytics dashboard is broken.
  • Political exposure: every vendor decision is potentially a news story. She can't afford to buy something that fails publicly.
  • Budget justification pressure: she has to walk into council and defend every line item. "Increases civic engagement" is not a defensible outcome — she needs numbers.

Behavior & Interests

Reads industry trades and LinkedIn daily. Belongs to the National Association of Government Communicators. Deeply values case studies, peer testimonials, and data — anything she can bring back to the mayor as evidence. Will spend time on a well-produced white paper. Will not read a listicle. Prefers email to cold calls, in-person demos to video pitches, and a peer introduction above all else. Mayors talk to mayors; communications directors talk to communications directors. A warm referral from one city to another is the single highest-converting lead source in her world.

Why Elena Buys In

Elena doesn't need software — she needs political cover and a measurable win. CivicCircle earns her attention by showing up in the places she already looks for legitimacy: a byline in GovTech, a panel at Code for America, a case study from a peer city with hard numbers. By the time she takes our first call, she has already read three pieces of our content, seen our logo next to two mayors she respects, and watched a ten-minute demo video on her own time. The sales conversation isn't a pitch — it's a shared planning session with someone she has already decided she trusts.

They are deeply engaged in their community and care what happens in it, but their bandwidth for finding things to do is zero. The shared family calendar is the single most important app on both of their phones.

Pain Points

  • The "I wish we'd known" regret: at least once a month, they hear about a free community event the Monday after it happened. "I would have taken the kids to that."
  • Fragmented school/civic/cultural information: the school uses one platform, the city uses another, the rec league uses a third, the library uses email. Nobody talks to each other.

  • Budget-consciousness: love free events. Resent paying for things their city should provide for free.

Behavior & Interests

Both heavy WhatsApp users (family group + neighborhood group). Priya checks Facebook community groups out of habit; Dev barely uses social media but reads the Nextdoor digest. Both rely on word-of-mouth from the school parent network. Strongly prefer free community events to paid ones. Deeply local in their loyalties — they'll drive twenty minutes to support a farmer they know by name, and they'll ignore a national chain three blocks away. Make most family-of-four decisions through Priya, who is the calendar keeper.

Why the Patels Buy In

The Patels don't need flash — they need trust and time savings. CivicCircle earns them by being free, ad-light, and locally operated, and by delivering content through channels they already rely on: the city's Friday email digest, the school parent Facebook group, the neighborhood WhatsApp. When Priya sees CivicCircle endorsed by the mayor, recommended by the elementary school PTA, and featured in her library's newsletter, the app goes from "another thing on my phone" to "the app my neighbors use." That is the only bar that matters in their household.

Section 2

Our Brand Story

In The New Rules of Marketing and PR, David Meerman Scott argues that brands no longer win on message — they win on story. Here is ours.

"The most important thing happening in your town right now is not on any app you own."

— The founding observation behind CivicCircle

2.1 The Origin

CivicCircle started from a frustration almost everyone has quietly experienced: hearing about a great event in your own town only after it happened. A neighborhood potluck the week after you moved in. A local band you would have driven twenty minutes to see, playing four blocks from your apartment. A council meeting that decided something you cared about and whose agenda nobody told you about.

These are not isolated missed events. They are symptoms of a structural failure. Local information — the kind that determines whether you feel connected to the place you live — has been shredded across a dozen platforms, each of which captures a slice of a community's life and none of which captures the whole. Big tech has given us infinite global feeds. It has given us almost nothing for the street we live on.

CivicCircle exists because someone needs to build the civic layer of the internet — the piece that puts what is happening in your town, in your week, in your pocket. A circle of information as wide as your community and no wider. A place where a mayor's town hall lives next to a local band's Tuesday show and a farmers market down the street, because those three things are all part of the same answer to the same question: what is happening around me, right now?

2.2 The Emotional Through-Line

Every brand needs an emotional center. Ours is one word: belonging.

Under every persona we serve is the same underlying need. Marcus, the newcomer, wants to feel like he lives somewhere, not just pays rent somewhere. Elena, the civic buyer, wants to feel that the work she puts into her city actually reaches the people who live in it. The Patel family wants their kids to grow up feeling rooted in a neighborhood where they recognize faces at the farmers market. None of these is a feature request. All of them are the same emotional request: make me feel like I belong to this place.

CivicCircle's brand voice follows from this. We are warm but professional. Civic but never bureaucratic. Specific — we say "your Tuesday" and "your block" and "your city" rather than "users" and "stakeholders." We take the mundane seriously, because the mundane is where civic life actually happens: a library lecture, a potluck, a local softball league, a Thursday council meeting. We treat these things like the cultural products they are.

2.3 What Makes Us Different

Three things set CivicCircle apart from every adjacent product, and our marketing reinforces all three at every opportunity.

01

We are civic, not commercial

Eventbrite treats events as inventory. Facebook treats them as engagement bait. We treat them as community infrastructure — which means we show the farmers market next to the stadium concert and the town hall next to the jazz show. No other platform does that.

02

We partner with cities, not around them

Our B2G model aligns our incentives with the public interest. A city pays us because civic engagement goes up — which means we cannot afford to optimize for the dark-pattern engagement metrics that dominate the rest of the internet.

03

Free for residents, always

A paywall at the resident level would make CivicCircle just another app. Keeping it free is a brand commitment, not a marketing tactic. Every piece of content we make reinforces it.

Section 3

The Inbound Marketing Plan

Our marketing strategy is organized around the four questions every inbound program must answer: what do we make, where do we put it, how do we amplify it using platforms that already exist, and why does this approach fit our customers?

3.1 Content Strategy — What We Make

We produce three categories of content, each mapped to a persona and a stage of the buyer journey. This is not a content calendar — it is a content system. Every piece fits into one of three buckets with a clear job to do.

Awareness

Civic Stories

For: Marcus & The Patels

Weekly short-form content celebrating the texture of local life. Instagram reels from a Saturday farmers market, a 90-second video of a neighborhood music venue's history, a photo essay of a town hall meeting. Built to be shared, not converted from.

  • "72 Hours in [City]: The Weekend Nobody Told You About"
  • "Meet the Person Who Runs Your Favorite Venue"
  • "What Actually Happens at a City Council Meeting"
Consideration

Practical Guides

For: All three personas

Medium-form, high-utility content designed to be searched for and saved. Things that answer real questions residents type into Google on a Friday afternoon. These are the pieces that win us SEO real estate for city-specific queries.

  • "The Complete Guide to Free Things to Do in [City] This Weekend"
  • "How to Plug Into Your New City (Even If You're an Introvert)"
  • "A Parent's Calendar: Every Family-Friendly Event This Month"
Decision (B2G)

Civic Case Studies

For: Elena

Long-form, data-rich pieces written for municipal buyers. Each one shows a real city, real numbers, and a real before/after. Designed to be printable, forwardable, and submittable as part of a procurement package.

  • "How Ridgemont Tripled Council Meeting Attendance in 90 Days"
  • "The Hidden ROI of Civic Engagement: A Municipal Leader's Playbook"
  • "Building the Digital Commons: A Framework for City Communications Directors"

3.2 Platforms — Where We Put It

3.3 Leverage — How We Use Platforms That Already Exist

A new startup cannot build its own audience from zero. We do not try. Our strategy is to deliberately attach ourselves to platforms, institutions, and communities that already have the attention we need. This is Scott's "new rules" principle in practice: earn attention through existing networks rather than buying it from scratch.

Municipal Co-Marketing

Every city contract includes a launch package that puts CivicCircle in the city's newsletter, social channels, and physical signage. One paid municipal contract immediately delivers tens of thousands of resident impressions at zero additional acquisition cost.

Venue & Festival Partnerships

Local venues and regional festivals — including New Orleans Jazz Fest-scale anchors — promote CivicCircle to their own audiences as their new ticketing and discovery layer. Their audience is our audience; we trade discovery surface for reach.

Meta Business API Integration

By integrating with Facebook's event infrastructure, we populate our feed with events that already have RSVP data — making CivicCircle feel alive from day one, even in a new market.

Local Press & Civic Media

Local newspapers, alt-weeklies, and civic newsletters are actively looking for positive tech-and-community stories. Our launch in every new market is a pitched earned-media moment, not a paid campaign.

Civic Tech Conference Circuit

Code for America, Smart City Expo, and state municipal association meetings are three-day concentrations of every Elena we need to reach. We do not exhibit at general tech conferences — we go where our buyer already goes.

School & PTA Networks

In every pilot market, partnering with one local school district or PTA unlocks the Priya Patel of that community — and the neighborhood networks she is connected to. One school partnership can outperform a month of paid social.

3.4 Why This Strategy Works for Our Customers

An inbound strategy is not a default — it is a choice that fits specific customers. CivicCircle's customer segments make inbound marketing not just effective but the only sensible choice for three reasons.

Our customers are skeptical of advertising.

Elena has watched a hundred vendor pitches. Priya has been burned by extractive apps. Marcus blocks ads by default. None of them will respond to a banner — all three will respond to content that respects their intelligence and their time. Inbound is how we earn an audience that doesn't trust advertising.

Our value is hard to explain in a 15-second ad.

"A civic engagement platform and local events app with a B2G subscription model" is not a hook. But a video showing a 90-year-old showing up to his first town hall because of a push notification — that is a story. Inbound lets us tell stories that long-form ads cannot.

Our customers trust institutions they already trust.

Priya trusts her PTA. Elena trusts the mayor of a neighboring city. Marcus trusts the podcast he listens to on his morning run. Inbound, done right, lets CivicCircle borrow the trust that these institutions have already built — rather than trying to build it alone.

Our model compounds, and inbound compounds with it.

Paid acquisition resets every month. Inbound accumulates. A blog post written today ranks for years. A satisfied city refers two more. A delighted resident installs the app for three friends. Our business model is a compounding network — our marketing strategy has to match.

Marketing Strategy

We Don't Pull People In. We Meet Them Where They Already Are.

CivicCircle's growth strategy is inbound by design. Following the frameworks developed by HubSpot and detailed in David Meerman Scott's The New Rules of Marketing and PR, we create content and experiences valuable enough that our customers find us — not the other way around.

01
Attract

Useful, civic-minded content that shows up when people search for what's happening locally.

02
Engage

Conversations that treat residents and city officials as neighbors, not leads.

03
Delight

An experience worth telling a friend — our most important acquisition channel.

Section 3

The Inbound Marketing Plan

Our marketing strategy is organized around the four questions every inbound program must answer: what do we make, where do we put it, how do we amplify it using platforms that already exist, and why does this approach fit our customers?

3.1 Content Strategy — What We Make

We produce three categories of content, each mapped to a persona and a stage of the buyer journey. This is not a content calendar — it is a content system. Every piece fits into one of three buckets with a clear job to do.

Awareness

Civic Stories

For: Marcus & The Patels

Weekly short-form content celebrating the texture of local life. Instagram reels from a Saturday farmers market, a 90-second video of a neighborhood music venue's history, a photo essay of a town hall meeting. Built to be shared, not converted from.

  • "72 Hours in [City]: The Weekend Nobody Told You About"
  • "Meet the Person Who Runs Your Favorite Venue"
  • "What Actually Happens at a City Council Meeting"
Consideration

Practical Guides

For: All three personas

Medium-form, high-utility content designed to be searched for and saved. Things that answer real questions residents type into Google on a Friday afternoon. These are the pieces that win us SEO real estate for city-specific queries.

  • "The Complete Guide to Free Things to Do in [City] This Weekend"
  • "How to Plug Into Your New City (Even If You're an Introvert)"
  • "A Parent's Calendar: Every Family-Friendly Event This Month"
Decision (B2G)

Civic Case Studies

For: Elena

Long-form, data-rich pieces written for municipal buyers. Each one shows a real city, real numbers, and a real before/after. Designed to be printable, forwardable, and submittable as part of a procurement package.

  • "How Ridgemont Tripled Council Meeting Attendance in 90 Days"
  • "The Hidden ROI of Civic Engagement: A Municipal Leader's Playbook"
  • "Building the Digital Commons: A Framework for City Communications Directors"

3.2 Platforms — Where We Put It

Every platform is chosen because a specific persona is already spending time there. We do not chase trends. We meet people where they are.

📸

Instagram

Primary: Marcus · Secondary: Priya

Reels, carousels, and city-specific stories. Location tags on every post. Collaboration posts with venues, artists, and farmers markets. Goal: become the Instagram handle every local culture account eventually follows back.

🎵

TikTok

Primary: Marcus

Short city-specific culture content. "A day in [City]" formats. Founder-led content with a human voice — people follow people, not logos.

📝

Blog & SEO

Primary: All personas

City-specific guides indexed for search terms like "things to do in [City] this weekend." This is our long-tail compounding asset — every article written once continues earning installs for years.

💼

LinkedIn

Primary: Elena

Long-form posts from the founding team on civic tech, community building, and public sector innovation. Case studies, white papers, and conference takeaways. The primary channel for B2G awareness.

📧

Email Newsletter

Primary: Priya · Secondary: Elena

A weekly Thursday-morning "This Weekend in [City]" digest. Low-friction, highly targeted, and exactly the format busy parents and comms directors actually read.

🎙️

Podcast Presence

Primary: Marcus · Secondary: Elena

Founder appearances on local culture podcasts and civic tech podcasts. Not a CivicCircle podcast — that's a vanity project. Guest spots that reach ready-made audiences.

3.3 Leverage — How We Use Platforms That Already Exist

A new startup cannot build its own audience from zero. We do not try. Our strategy is to deliberately attach ourselves to platforms, institutions, and communities that already have the attention we need. This is Scott's "new rules" principle in practice: earn attention through existing networks rather than buying it from scratch.

Municipal Co-Marketing

Every city contract includes a launch package that puts CivicCircle in the city's newsletter, social channels, and physical signage. One paid municipal contract immediately delivers tens of thousands of resident impressions at zero additional acquisition cost.

Venue & Festival Partnerships

Local venues and regional festivals — including New Orleans Jazz Fest-scale anchors — promote CivicCircle to their own audiences as their new ticketing and discovery layer. Their audience is our audience; we trade discovery surface for reach.

Meta Business API Integration

By integrating with Facebook's event infrastructure, we populate our feed with events that already have RSVP data — making CivicCircle feel alive from day one, even in a new market.

Local Press & Civic Media

Local newspapers, alt-weeklies, and civic newsletters are actively looking for positive tech-and-community stories. Our launch in every new market is a pitched earned-media moment, not a paid campaign.

Civic Tech Conference Circuit

Code for America, Smart City Expo, and state municipal association meetings are three-day concentrations of every Elena we need to reach. We do not exhibit at general tech conferences — we go where our buyer already goes.

School & PTA Networks

In every pilot market, partnering with one local school district or PTA unlocks the Priya Patel of that community — and the neighborhood networks she is connected to. One school partnership can outperform a month of paid social.
